Network capital, social networks, and travel: an empirical illustration from Concepción, Chile

Juan Antonio Carrasco

Department of Civil Engineering, Universidad de Concepción, PO Box 160-C, Concepción, Chile; e-mail: j.carrasco@udec.cl

Beatriz Cid-Aguayo

Department of Sociology and Anthropology, Universidad de Concepción, PO Box 160-C, Concepción, Chile; e-mail: beatrizcid@udec.cl
Received 31 May 2010; in revised form 6 June 2011

Abstract. The recent interest in the role of transport in social interactions has been partly motivated by the hope of achieving a better understanding of the role that transport plays in network capital, or the resources that people can gather from their social networks. Despite the relevancy of this question for policy, little empirical work has been done to elucidate this relationship. This paper illustrates a method of data collection and analysis, using the social networks approach to assess the role of transport in social support, both emotional and material. The personal networks of two neighbourhoods in Concepción, Chile, with different income levels, are compared, focusing on the role of car ownership in network capital, and the relevance of the time and space characteristics of social contacts. The results suggest that having a car at home does not lead to homogeneous access to network capital, which depends heavily on the kind of social resource studied and the income context (neighbourhood) in which the individuals are embedded. The findings also underscore the need to treat social capital as a truly multidimensional concept and the usefulness of the personal networks approach for assessing these complex relationships.

Keywords: travel behaviour, social networks, network capital

  1. Introduction

In recent years, travel behaviour researchers have broadened their perspectives beyond microeconomic paradigms, expanding their theories and models through activity-based paradigms (Chapin, 1974), recognizing that travel is generally derived from the need to perform activities. This approach has allowed substantial advances in data collection, analysis, and the modelling of urban travel behaviour (Buliung and Kanaroglou, 2007).

However, these individualistic explanations are limited in scope, especially when questions about the role of transport in accessibility to social networks arise (Buliung and Kanaroglou, 2007). By studying social networks, travel behaviour researchers expect not only to improve their current models, but also to have a better understanding of the impacts of transport policies on broader societal issues such as social inclusion and social capital. For example, there is an increasing need to assess the role of distance and travel on the exchange of support—both emotional and economic—between individuals (Axhausen, 2005).

Although there is speculative discussion about the potential relevance of transport in social networks and social capital (Currie and Stanley, 2008; Stanley and Vella-Brodrick, 2009), the evidence is still scarce. Furthermore, this empirical work has been performed in the context of developed countries, where most of the population owns a car, and where mobility issues are related mostly to ‘car dependence’: that is, the reliance on cars to perform daily activities (Johnson et al, 2010).

This paper focuses on the intertwined relationships between social relations (networks) and social support (capital), considering their temporal, spatial, and travel context. The paper addresses the question of the relevance of car ownership on the spatial and temporal patterns of networks, and their relationship with people’s access to social support resources. To answer this question, a method of data collection and analysis based on the social networks approach is used as a tool for assessing the relevance of transport (cars) in social capital. The empirical setting is Concepción, Chile, a city where a minority of the population owns a car, and most people rely on public transport and nonmotorised modes to perform their daily activities. From a policy perspective, this question is situated in the context of neoliberal policies in Chile which have given preference to private rather than public transport and nonmotorised modes (Zegras, 2003). In fact, most of the recent transport infrastructure investment in Chile has promoted car use through the construction and enhancement of urban highways, contrasting with very limited financial support to the public transport system (Figueroa, 2005; Muñoz et al, 2008).

The rest of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 presents a brief theoretical review of key concepts developed herein: social networks, network capital, and the role of transport. Section 3 reviews the data and statistical methods employed in the study, while section 4 reviews the main results. Section 5 presents the conclusions, suggesting future lines of research.

  1. Theoretical review

Research about the role of transport and social capital is challenged by the heterogeneous definition of social capital (eg, Portes, 1998). Bearing these issues in mind, this section first briefly reviews the concepts of social networks and network capital, and then discusses recent elaborations regarding the relationship between social networks and transport.

  1. Operational definition of social networks and network capital

The concept of social capital has gained increasing importance given its potential policy implications in a variety of contexts: ‘positive’ communities provide the benefit of social capital to their members. However, the definition and scope of social capital varies substantially among authors; extensive reviews and discussions of the concept are available in the literature (Fischer, 2005; Lin, 1999; 2008; Portes, 1998; Putnam, 2000). This paper builds on Bourdieu’s definition of social capital as “potential resources which are linked to the possession of a durable network of more or less institutionalized relationships of mutual acquaintance or recognition” (1986, page 248), constituting one of the forms of capital whose distribution contributes to the reproduction of social structure. As expressed by Lin, these are “resources embedded in one’s social networks, resources that can be accessed or mobilized through ties in networks” (2008, page 71), which can be seen as an individualistic approach to social capital. These definitions are preferred over community and institutional based definitions, due to their consistency with the personal social network perspective, as will be explained.

The social network perspective adopted in this paper is more than metaphorical, drawing on four decades of findings in sociology and other disciplines (Wellman and Berkowitz, 1988). In general terms, the network approach focuses on one dimension of social structure, according to Tindall and Wellman (2001), who state that

  • [social network analysis] conceives social structure as a social network, that is a set of actors (nodes) and a set of relationships connecting pairs of these actors; the actors can be groups, organizations or even nation-states as well as persons, and the relationships are flows of resources that reflect relations of control, dependence, and cooperation. Network analysis’ core concern is to understand how social structures facilitate and constrain opportunities, behaviours, and cognitions. Network analysts investigate patterns of relationships that connect members of social systems, and how these patterns channel resources to specific locations in social structures” (pages 266–267).

The social networks approach emphasizes the potential resources mobilized through social networks (Phillipson et al, 2004), a definition that is highly consistent with the focus by Bourdieu and Lin on resources rather than places. As Wellman and Frank (2001) state, “the loosely-coupled, networked nature of contemporary societies means that social capital comes contingently from a variety of persons, ties and networks, rather than stably from a single, soldiery group” (page 234), not only emphasizing the importance of strong, dense, reciprocal, and formal normative ties in social capital—as Coleman (1988) does—but also of weaker ties, which potentially provide access to a rich array of resources (Granovetter, 1973). Lin’s definition also broadens the spatial scope of social networks and the mobilization of resources, acknowledging that urban contexts fosters more culturally diverse social relations (Fischer, 1982), and that support communities are physically dispersed, departing from more locally geographic definitions of social capital, grounded in localized communities (eg, see Putnam, 2000). This definition also acknowledges that social capital is a multidimensional construct, involving several and different kinds of resources (emotional and material), which are predominantly derived from people’s social contacts (Owen and Videras, 2009).

In order to combine the social capital and social network approaches, this paper uses the concept of network capital, defined by Wellman and Frank (2000) as the form of social capital that makes resources available through interpersonal ties, which assumes that the provision of network capital depends on the characteristics of each network member (or alter) and that the relational characteristics of each tie with a network member. From a travel behaviour perspective, the network capital approach reflects the variety of ties and spatial locations of network members, highlighting the role of transport in the development and maintenance of communities. As a consequence, the analysis broadens the definition of ‘community’ in terms of space (eg, beyond neighbours), support exchange (eg, economic but also emotional), or relationships (eg, beyond family and neighbours ties).

  1. Review of past efforts: the link between social networks, transport, and network capital

The study of the explicit impacts of transport on social networks is still in its early stages (Dugundji et al, 2008). Despite earlier studies that speculated on how the urban experience can be ‘narrowed’ by transport through separating communities from workplaces and insulating the world in between (Yago, 1983), more recent literature acknowledges the complex role of transport in urban social interactions (Axhausen, 2000). For example, Wellman (2001) argues that transportation, communication, and computer networks can be considered to be manifestations of the social networks that connect people with each other as well as being factors that expand the geographic scope and size of the social networks. In fact, as Urry (2002; 2003) argues, social sciences would need a ‘mobility turn’ that could concentrate further on the role of transport to acknowledge the roles (both positive and negative) that physical and corporeal travel plays in social life. This mobility turn would require not only a more solid theoretical framework, but it would also need better data to support an understanding of the role of transport in social life (Axhausen, 2005). Disentangling how transport influences social networks constitutes an important challenge for the discipline of travel behaviour, considering that current individualistic paradigms and tools do not account for the complexity of social activities and travel in urban cities (Axhausen, 2008).

In this context the role of social capital in travel behaviour—and network capital in particular—has gained attention, being recognized as a broader policy goal that may be achieved through the provision of accessibility and transport facilities (eg, Stanley and Vella-Brodrick, 2009). Urry (2002), for example, argues that fully active and engaged members of society require social capital within localities, and that the participation of such individuals involves transportation and mobility. In the same vein, Currie and Stanley (2008) claim that the role of transport in social capital has been overlooked, pointing out the need for a better understanding of how transport acts to address social disadvantage through the provision of mobility. In fact, these authors identify ‘plausible links’ between transport and ‘positive’ social interaction, such as the role of car dependence, spatial relations, copresence, and well-being. However, the literature still tends to focus mostly on theoretical developments (eg, Doi et al, 2008). As Stanley and Vella-Brodick (2009) note, little theoretical work has been undertaken on social capital and transport which could recognize the need for more explicit measures regarding social capital and community, and the role of social networks. In fact, as recent empirical efforts suggest, there is still a broad scope for research to quantify and identify the relevance of transport in people’s network capital (eg, Farber and Páez, 2009; Hartell, 2008).

In this context the social network analysis employed in this paper seeks to complement the current visions of social capital in the urban and transport planning literature by studying the explicit context in which social exchange occurs in the context of travel behaviour.

  1. Data and methods

From a practical viewpoint, collecting data about complete social networks in cities is unfeasible, since it requires a complete list of each relationship, which is very difficult to obtain in urban areas. To overcome this issue, this study employed personal or egocentric social networks or ‘my network’ for any given individual, where the respondent is referred to as ‘ego’ and all of the people mentioned by the respondent are referred to as ‘alters’ (Marsden, 2005).

  1. The “Communities in Concepción” study

The data employed in this paper come from the study “Communities in Concepción” (August 2008–April 2009), which focused on the characteristics of social activity–travel through the analysis of personal networks in different neighbourhoods of Concepción, Chile. The city is located 500 km south of the country’s capital, Santiago, and its metropolitan area has a population of around one million people, being the second largest in the country. The city covers about 300 km2 and is served by a relatively good-quality mass-transit operating system and supporting infrastructure. Concepción presents diverse income levels, and around 35% of all households own a car.

  1. Study area

Data about personal networks were collected in two distinctive city neighbourhoods: Agüita de la Perdiz, composed by lower–medium-income households (hereafter, LI), and La Virgen, composed by higher–medium income residences (hereafter, HI). LI was created in 1950 by a land occupation performed by politically organized pobladores who constructed their own neighbourhood, which then gave good levels of social organization. In its earliest years it looked like a shantytown; however, after sixty years of continuous occupation, and thanks to the self-construction and the public effort of urbanization, it has become an average lower–middle-class neighbourhood. HI was built in two stages. First, around the 1950s, two important city companies built houses for their professional staff. Later, during the 1970s and 1980s, the liberal professional and upper middle class built new houses in the area, consolidating the neighbourhood as it currently looks.

The households of LI and HI are not extremely poor or wealthy; spatially, these two neighbourhoods are near each other and have good accessibility (around twenty minutes walking distance) to downtown Concepción, where most of the services and workplaces are located. In fact, the neighbourhoods were chosen to explore two different social and income contexts while controlling for spatial access to urban facilities. Figure 1 shows the locations of the neighbourhoods and of each respondent in the city.

As expected from the income difference between these neighbourhoods, only 30% of the respondents living in LI have a car at home, which contrasts with 85% in HI. However, within each neighbourhood there are no relevant differences in the relationship between income and household car ownership, which is a very useful result to control for the effect of these two variables on the access to network capital.

The two neighbourhoods also present very different patterns in terms of the mode of transport used for social visits. In fact, the proportion of car use is 22% in LI versus 72% in HI, whereas the proportion of public transport use is 72% in LI and 36% in HI. Walking was mentioned as a usual transport mode for visits by 35% of the respondents living in LI and 28% of those residing in HI. The similar shares of walking for visits also highlight the similarities between the neighbourhoods in terms of their urban context (ie, walkability).

  1. Data-collection method

The data were collected in semiguided interviews of 120 people (60 from LI and 60 from HI); a total of 2669 personal network members were reported (2500 were usable answers). Respondents were chosen by a stratified random sampling procedure, considering sex, age, and occupational status as key variables, preserving their marginal distributions according to the Chilean Census data.

The method employed to elicit the personal networks was the name generator technique, which has been used extensively in sociology (Marsden, 2005) and, recently, in travel behaviour (Hogan et al, 2007; Larsen et al, 2006). The instrument is based on Carrasco et al (2008) and concentrates on the individual’s affective network, or those people defined as emotionally close; this approach has been shown to be useful for understanding social activity–travel patterns (Carrasco and Miller, 2006). Concretely, respondents named people who lived outside their household to whom they felt emotionally very close and somewhat close. The category ‘very close’ consisted of “people with whom you discuss important matters, or with whom you regularly keep in touch, or they are there for you if you need help”. The category ‘somewhat close’ consisted of “more than just casual acquaintances, but not very close people”. This emotional ‘closeness’ approach defines two aspects: strength of the tie (strong versus somewhat strong), and personal network ‘boundary’, excluding casual acquaintances.

Besides gathering data on sociodemographic attributes of respondents (egos), other aspects include alters’ age, relationship, gender, home location, and most frequent place of interaction with the ego. In addition, information was compiled about the communication and interaction patterns (face-to-face, socializing, telephone, e-mail) between the egos and each of the alters. The data also include personal network composition, involving the number of alters in terms of sociodemographics (eg, age, gender) and ego–alter relationship (eg, family member, friend). Respondents also recorded the connections and strength they believed to exist among their alters, which were used to study social network measures that have been found relevant in the past in similar data (Carrasco and Miller, 2009). These are: size (number of alters); density (ratio between the number of ties present in the network and the maximum possible); number of isolates (alters connected only to the ego); number of components (subgraphs that are connected within, but disconnected between, subgraphs, including isolates); number of cliques (defined as the maximal complete subgraph of three or more nodes); mean degree (mean number of ties linking each node); and mean Freeman’s betweenness (which accounts for the mean shortest paths between nodes). More information about these measures can be found in Freeman (1979) and Wasserman and Faust (1994).

Finally, respondents were asked to identify both directions of social support: “to whom (from their networks) they have given or could give help” and “from whom (from their networks) they have received or could receive help”. Several support dimensions were collected, four of which were used in this paper:

(1) emotional: discussing important matters,

(2) monetary: giving/receiving small loans,

(3) mobility: driving someone to work/shopping or for emergencies,

(4) employment: offering access to job opportunities.

  1. Multivariate multilevel models

The dataset has a bilevel, hierarchical structure. The first level involves the ego–alter relationship as well as the alter’s personal characteristics. The second level involves measurements of each network as a whole and descriptive data about the egos. Analyzing differences between alters cannot be done using standard regression because such data violate the assumption of independence between ego–alter cases. Hence, hierarchical linear modeling—which controls for variations between networks and between alters—is employed (Van Duijn et al, 1999).

  1. Analysis and results
  2. Personal network characteristics

Mann–Whitney nonparametric tests were employed to test statistical differences between the personal network characteristics from the two neighbourhoods (Milton and Arnold, 2003).

  1. Network size

From a network capital viewpoint, size is very important since more alters involve potential access to more social resources. Figure 2 shows that—with the exception of a few very large networks in HI [‘power networkers’ according to Boase et al (2006)]—the size distributions are very similar, with a mean of 21 in LI and of 23 in HI; this difference is statistically nonsignificant. Similarly, the medians are 19 in LI and 21 in HI, and even at the 75th percentile, network sizes are 28 and 26, respectively. These overall values are very similar to those found using similar survey instruments in other countries such as Canada (Carrasco et al, 2008), Switzerland (Larsen et al, 2006), and the Netherlands (Tillema et al, 2008). Although they may be partially determined by the instrument (the name generator), there seems to be a cognitive and/or social threshold that deserves to be further studied. More importantly for this paper, this similarity of network sizes is very intriguing, considering the generalized assumption in the literature that higher income egos tend to have larger social networks (Boase et al, 2006; Carrasco and Miller, 2009).

  1. Network composition

Table 1 presents the mean value of the proportions of alters who have common characteristics in each network. Besides some differences in age, most of the other sociodemographic characteristics are very similar. In particular, the mean proportion of alters with a relationship lasting longer than ten years is very similar between both neighbourhoods, suggesting very stable core contacts within these two neighbourhoods.

Despite these similarities, there are relevant differences in terms of the share of ego–alter relationships in the personal networks. In fact, the mean proportion of immediate family members in LI is double that in HI, and the mean proportion of neighbours in LI is four times that in HI. In contrast, the proportion of friends in LI is around half that in HI. Note that these proportions do not add up to 100% since the data instrument allowed respondents to identify alters with more than one role (eg, both neighbours and family members). In addition, the number of people from ‘formal’ organizations—in Putnam’s sense (2000)—is very low in these networks, narrowing the probability of getting support from this source.

  1. Network measures

As table 2 shows, personal networks in LI have a statistically significant higher density and mean degree compared with those from HI. Conversely, egos living in HI reported twice as many isolates as their counterparts in LI. These results are consistent with the previous results of network composition in LI, since immediate family alters may have more connections between them than friends. At the same time, the level of subgrouping is very similar between both neighbourhoods, considering that the difference in the number of components is mainly due to the number of isolates, and the similarity in the number of cliques among them.

  1. Network spatial distribution

As table 3 shows, personal networks in LI are more local, with a striking relevance of the neighbourhood (mean proportion of 44%) compared with the networks in HI (mean proportion of 11%). In contrast, the mean proportion of alters in the city (outside the neighbourhood) for HI is almost double that of LI, suggesting that higher income egos can maintain their contacts over greater spatial scopes. In fact, besides the similarities in the mean contacts in the province—possibly due to low-income egos having relatives dwelling in the countryside—personal networks in HI tend to have a greater number of alters at the national and international scale. Then, the potential social resources in LI are much more local than in HI.

  1. Social resources

The comparison of different social resources in each network shows a higher mean proportion of alters able to provide social resources for people living in HI compared with those living in LI (table 4). This tendency occurs not only for material resources (such as lending small amounts of money), but also for emotional resources (such as giving advice). In addition, the results suggest imbalances between giving and receiving support, suggesting nonsymmetric reciprocity processes; a result that has been found elsewhere (Plickert et al, 2007).

Then, from an aggregated perspective, egos living in HI have a higher potential of securing social resources through their personal networks, both in terms of the number of alters who can provide support, and in terms of the variety of resources that can be provided.

  1. Multivariate analysis

The previous findings serve as a context for disaggregated multivariate models that focus on the relevance of each ego–alter relationship and how potential social support exchanges are related with time (frequency of interaction), space (distance between alters), and access to cars.

A key aim of this analysis is to study the relevance of car ownership in support exchanges. As discussed before, although respondents with higher incomes are more likely to have a car at home than their counterparts, within each neighbourhood there is reasonable variability in order to control both for incomes (ie, the neighbourhood context) and for car ownership. Thus, all the following models use ‘car–neighbourhood’ variables which capture the following four possible situations:

  • no car availability and living in LI (reference category),
  • car availability and living in LI,
  • no car availability and living in HI,
  • car availability and living in HI.

Two sets of models are presented. The first set consists of two models that explore the relevance of car ownership and other personal network variables in time (ego–alter frequency of face-to-face interaction) and space (ego–alter distance). In the second set, four models explore the relevance of car ownership and other personal network variables in the potential ego–alter support exchange.

  1. Multivariate models for the role of car ownership in time and space

Table 5 presents an ordinal multilevel model in which the dependent variable is the ordinal ego–alter frequency of interaction. The structure of this model is similar to others reported in the literature, taking the ego–alter distance as the control variable, based on previous experiences from the literature (eg, Carrasco and Miller, 2009). The model results show that the ego–alter frequency of interaction tends to decay with increasing distances (Mok et al, 2010). The results also suggest that neither income nor car ownership plays a relevant role in the ego–alter frequencies of interaction.

Table 6 complements the previous analysis, presenting an ordinal multilevel model in which the dependent variable is the ego–alter distance. This model is similar in structure to others found in the literature, taking the ego–alter distance as the control variable, based on previous experiences from the literature (Carrasco and Miller, 2009). The results confirm previous findings in the literature showing that immediate family members and friends tend to locate spatially closer than other social contacts such as members of organizations and coworkers (the reference categories). In terms of the relevance of car ownership, the model shows that the spatial distribution of personal networks is driven mainly by income rather than car availability.

  1. Multivariate models for social support

After reviewing the impact of having a car over the interaction frequency and the spatiality of personal networks, the following models analyze the impact of car ownership, income, ego–alter relationship, and other sociodemographic variables in the social support exchange.

Table 7 presents the results of four multivariate multilevel models focused on four different types of resources: emotional support (M1), small money loans (M2), driving someone to activities (M3), and advice on job opportunities (M4). The coefficients finally considered in the models are those with a t-test greater than 1.0 (around 85% degree of confidence).

Overall, all models present statistically significant coefficients in their random part, showing that the model structure captures appropriately the variances within each ego–network level and ego–alter level.

  1. M1: Emotional support The results suggest that the geography of alters impacts emotional support only when distances are very great. In fact, social contacts living outside the province (in the country or abroad) are less likely to give or receive emotional support than those living closer, with no differences in the probability of ego–alter emotional support exchange for most local categories (ie, neighbourhood, city, and Greater Concepción Area).

As expected intuitively, immediate family members are more likely to give or receive emotional support, although friends also have a higher propensity to help than other relationships (eg, members from organizations and coworkers). In the case of age, younger egos and alters tend to receive or give less emotional support than their older counterparts.

Regarding car ownership, respondents with a car at home have a higher likelihood of receiving or giving emotional support, especially those living in HI. Regarding people living in LI, owning a car is positively related to giving emotional support, although not to receiving it; however, the statistical effect is weak. Therefore, people who do not own a car in both neighbourhoods have a relatively lower probability of having exchanges of emotional support. In other words, having a car could facilitate the exchange of emotional support, although with some differences between the neighbourhoods. Yet, the results from M1 also have to be understood in the context of the empirical findings from the previous sections of the frequency and spatiality of contacts. Thus, car ownership would not be related to frequency of interaction or spatial scope of networks, but possibly to greater time–space flexibility to reach these contacts. Further research is needed to explore this hypothesis.

  1. M2: Small money loans Regarding monetary support, egos are less likely to exchange monetary help with alters living outside their neighbourhood, especially if they live outside the city. This effect becomes even sharper with those alters living abroad. In addition, immediate family relationships have more probability of exchanging monetary loans, while friendship does not seem to be differentiating with respect to reference categories (such as coworkers and members from organizations), similarly to Wellman and Wortley (1990).

Respondents owning a car and living in HI are more likely to give and receive monetary support than the reference group. Although statistically weak, a similar trend can be seen for egos who live in HI and that do not have a car. In this sense, and as intuitively expected, income seems to be much more relevant for the likelihood of receiving monetary help than car ownership.

Finally, within the LI neighbourhood, having a car means a lower probability of receiving money loans with respect to those who do not own a car, probably since having a car is related with the availability of resources.

  1. M3: Car access for working/shopping or for emergencies The geography of car access shows that the longer the ego–alter distance, the lower the likelihood of a car support exchange. This result is consistent with the intuition that car support exchanges are dependent on the availability of having the resource near and, thus, are directly dependent on the ego–alter distance. Similar to money loans, only immediate family relationships between the ego and alter involve a higher probability of car support exchange; there are no effects of friendship with respect to the reference category.

In terms of the relevance of car ownership, and as expected, those respondents in each neighbourhood who own a car are more likely to provide temporal access to this resource than those who do not, although the effect is somewhat weak statistically for the respondents living in LI. Having a car in LI also implies less probability of receiving this kind of help compared with the HI context. Although this result was statistically weaker, it suggests that living in an environment in which cars are a scarce resource involves lower chances of receiving that kind of support. On the other hand, respondents in HI are more likely to have temporal access to cars than in LI even if they do own a car, a result that could be related to the greater availability of cars in higher income contexts.

  1. M4: Job opportunities Similar to M1, this model shows a small effect of ego–alter distances, suggesting a positive effect only for those alters living in the city or the Greater Concepción Area. This result may be intuitive since alters living at greater distances are not necessarily aware of job opportunities in the area of interest; conversely, those living in the neighbourhood do not necessarily have the diversity of contacts required to provide new job opportunities.

Regarding the relevance of car ownership, a first contrast between the two neighbourhoods for respondents who do not own a car, egos living in HI are more likely to give or receive support regarding job opportunities than egos living in LI.

Second, when comparing the respondents living in LI, those with a car available tend to have a higher probability of giving advice on job opportunities than those who do not own a car. This result may imply that having a car in LI means a ‘better position’ to give advice on jobs than those who do not own a car due to income and mobility. On the other hand, respondents living in HI have a higher probability of giving advice on job opportunities than of receiving it, regardless of their access to cars, which could suggest the primacy of a socioeconomic effect: that is, better access to job opportunities because of their income context and less need to receive this kind of help. In sum, owning a car in LI means a differential on job opportunities that does not show in HI.

  1. Conclusions and future work

The paper has presented an analysis of the relationship between transport (car ownership) and network capital, using the personal networks approach. This methodology proved to be a useful perspective for capturing empirically the role of transport in social support exchanges, providing the opportunity to study the relevance of time and space in social contacts. The rich dataset employed herein is capable of capturing the respondents’ personal network and their spatial and temporal characteristics (ego–alter distances and frequency of interactions), serving as a ‘natural’ framework with which to study the role of transport in network capital.

The empirical illustration concentrated on studying the role of car ownership by comparing the network capital levels between two neighbourhoods with different income averages but similar accessibility to jobs and urban services. The first step compared personal network characteristics to assess the variety and quality of access to potential resources. The results suggest that, despite their similar average sizes, there were differences in their composition, tie–node structure, and spatiality.

Personal network characteristics in time and space were studied as a second step towards understanding ego–alter flows of support, conceiving car ownership as a potential facilitator of space and time constraints for social exchanges. The results suggest that car ownership does not directly influence the frequencies of ego–alter social interaction, and that personal network spatiality is influenced mainly by income, not by car ownership.

In the final step we studied the relevance of car ownership to network capital, showing differences depending on the type of social support. The results suggest that having a car seems to be more relevant for emotional support than for material resources, which seem to be driven mainly by income. Then, the role of car ownership in network capital is mediated by the individual’s income context, emphasizing the need to acknowledge the multidimensionality of social support.

All these results highlight recent warnings in the literature about overly simplistic indicators (eg, mere car ownership) related to the role of transport on social exclusion (Johnson et al, 2010). In fact, the empirical findings support the view that having a car at home does not necessarily imply a difference in people’s network capital. Furthermore, they caution about the complexity of assessing the role of transport in social capital, considering the differences in the spatial and temporal configuration of personal networks between the two neighbourhoods. For example, the locality of the personal networks of low-income egos may suggest an adaptation process due to their lower levels of accessibility and a potential variety of social resources.

In addition, distinguishing between the relevance of car and income in social support exchange could contribute to the debate about boosting network capital through transport policies. At a first view, the secondary role of transport in personal network characteristics and their embedded social support levels would suggest a much more central role for redistributive policies compared with transport policies. The exception seems to be the relevance of cars in emotional support, possibly due to the capability of cars to offer better time–space convenience. Yet, these results also suggest that people living in LI may have adapted to lower levels of accessibility to people, due to the higher space–time constraints they experience using public transport, especially considering their lack in the variety of resources both in space (mostly local) and types or relationships (mostly family). This issue is particularly relevant in the Chilean context, where most of the population depend on public transport to perform their activities, which contrasts with government policies that have historically prioritized investment in private transport. In this sense, the provision of better spatial access to social networks through transport needs to be articulated with other policies addressing social vulnerability. In the same vein, the focus of these policies should be towards lower income groups (and then public transport), which have much lower network capital levels in the context studied.

From a research perspective, future efforts should concentrate on further disentangling the effects of income and car in social support, which do not necessarily affect all types of social support evenly. Beyond income, the relevance of the urban context and the role of public transport need to be studied, going beyond neighbourhoods with good public transport coverage and access to the city’s main amenities. In addition, these effects need to be studied in different national contexts to assess other particularities which are difficult to measure explicitly with the methods employed in the paper.

In sum, although transport seems to have different impacts depending on the type of social support, its magnitude and relevance are still open questions. Nonetheless, the social network methods and theory presented in this paper constitute a useful approach for capturing the multidimensionality of network capital, providing a good starting point from which to capture the complexities of the individual’s social context, both in space and in time, and their link with transport policies.
